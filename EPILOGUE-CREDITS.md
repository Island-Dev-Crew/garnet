# Epilogue: After the Wave

*"He who has ears to hear, let him hear."* — Matthew 11:15 (KJV)

---

In March of 2026, the story in these pages caught up to where Jon's life actually was. It was a sprint — get the truth down, get it published, get it into people's hands. Accuracy would come later, if it came at all.

Some things were true in the story. Some things were only partially true. All of it was pointing toward something bigger.

What happened between April 11 and April 17, 2026, was not part of the original plan.

Jon was laying in bed on a Saturday night — the Sabbath had just ended, the week was about to start again — when he heard someone describe the beauty of the Ruby programming language. How the code reads. How it flows. How it feels human. He thought about how God is the same way: organized, orderly, beautiful. And then he wondered why everyone was talking about Rust, and whether the two could be reconciled.

He wrote one paragraph to an AI, asking a question he didn't expect would matter.

The AI spent fifty-eight minutes reading eight hundred sources. It came back with something that changed the direction of the company.

Six days later, that paragraph became a programming language.

Not a prototype. Not a sketch. A working, tested, cross-platform language system with 1,244 tests, 136 security validations, 22,000 lines of source code, seven research papers, and a language specification that could be submitted to MIT for review.

It was built during a window when his utilities had been cut off twice in the same month. When his mother asked him where the money was, and he didn't have an answer. When his Claude Max subscription was the most expensive thing he owned, and it was about to expire.

He told the AI: *"This is going to be presented at MIT. Scrutinized by people who know more than me. Miss nothing."*

It didn't miss.

The language is called Garnet. A gemstone formed under pressure. Compressed. Hardened. Beautiful because of the force, not despite it.

The logo is half Rust — for safety, rigor, and systems integrity — and half Ruby — for elegance, expressiveness, and human-centered beauty — with a memory core at the center. Because the thing that makes Garnet different isn't the syntax. It's that the language itself understands what agents need: working memory, episodic memory, semantic memory, procedural memory. Declared like variables. Enforced by the compiler.

When someone asked why he built a programming language instead of a CRUD app like everyone else, his answer was simple: *"Most agencies build CRUD apps. IDC is designing a programming language."*

He didn't say it to be impressive. He said it because it was true.

The money was still gone. The utilities had still been cut off. The company was still trying to breathe. But something had shifted.

He had gone from telling his mother about a vision he couldn't explain to showing her a thing that ran on two different operating systems, passed more than a thousand tests, and came with seven papers proving it was real.

She still asked where the money was.

He still didn't have an answer.

But he had Garnet. And that was more than he had on Saturday.
