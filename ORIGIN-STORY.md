## The Spark

**Saturday, April 11, 2026. Showered. Naked. Laying on the bed.**

I'd just gotten out of the shower, still drying off, hadn't put clothes on yet. On my way to my mother's for Sabbath lunch with the family. And I was listening to someone describe the beauty of Ruby — how it makes code feel natural, readable, almost human. And it clicked: that's what God is like. Organized. Orderly. Beautiful. Not chaotic.

Then the question formed: *everybody's talking about Rust right now — but what would it look like to reconcile Rust and Ruby together?*

I picked up my phone and asked Claude. Not Pulse — because Pulse checks me when I ask things on Sabbath and I wasn't about to have that conversation again. 🤣 I snuck around my own assistant to get answers without feeling guilty.

I didn't need work done — just a research question. Since I was on the Max plan expiring soon, I might as well use it.

Claude spent 58 minutes across 800+ sources. By the time I got clothes on, left the house, and drove to my mother's, the research was done and waiting. As I read it at Sabbath lunch, I realized: *oh, this is something pretty substantial.*

That one question, asked naked on a Saturday morning, became Garnet.

---

## The Research Chain

I didn't start coding. I started researching. And I didn't trust just one AI to tell me if the idea was real — so I built a consensus council.

**1. Claude (first pass)** — I sent a one-paragraph question into research mode on the way to Sabbath lunch. It spent 58 minutes thinking across 800+ sources before returning its answer. By the time I was at my mother's table, it was finished. The verdict: there's a real gap here. It's viable. Nobody has done this.

**2. ChatGPT (extended thinking)** — I took Claude's results and pressure-tested them against GPT's reasoning. It expanded the surface area, found edges I hadn't considered, and pushed back where it needed to.

**3. Grok 4.2 (multi-agent research)** — I wanted the public-facing perspective. What does the broader ecosystem think? Grok brought sentiment analysis, community awareness, and insight into what the market would actually accept.

**4. Back to Claude (gap validation)** — I returned to Claude with all three perspectives. It identified what was still missing, what still had to be true before any of this could be real, and started filling in the gaps.

**5. Gemini Deep Research** — I almost forgot about Gemini. But Gemini had just finished a quantum memory study, so I sent everything through Gemini's deep research pipeline. It surfaced multiple novelty ideas I hadn't thought of, especially around the memory architecture. Gemini's research base on the memory side was far more substantial than anything the other three surfaced.

**6. Back to Claude (final synthesis)** — Claude had been carrying all the context from every previous pass. It synthesized everything — all six perspectives, all the research, all the gap analyses — into one coherent, buildable architecture.

This wasn't one AI telling me "great idea!" It was five models, seven passes, and genuine adversarial pressure-testing before I wrote a single line of code.

I told every model the same thing: *"This is going to be presented at MIT. Scrutinized by people who know more than me. Miss nothing."*

They all went to work knowing that standard.

## The Build Window

By the time I moved into Claude Code, the architecture was fully specified. Every gap was filled. Every question had been answered. I had the entire build plan laid out in Phase 1 of Plan Mode.

I was running on Claude Max ($100/month → $200/month). I stayed up all night Thursday because I thought the subscription would expire at midnight. I pushed through Phase 1, finalized the plan, built the structure. The nights were the hardest part — no sleep, then straight to work the next day. But I kept going because the window was closing.

**Then Claude Opus 4.7 dropped.**

I updated the app three times (they kept changing services). Anthropic extended usage limits to support the new model. And I already had the full spec built out.

So I told it: *"Go back to the beginning. Review the plan. Check for gaps. Fill anything that needs filling. Continue to enhance. Let me know if there are insights I need to be aware of."*

Opus 4.7 ran with 1 million context and max thinking. I was screen-capturing everything — how the model was working, how it moved from 4.6 to 4.7, how it processed the entire plan in one shot.

The build happened fast because the *thinking* was already done. Six research passes. A complete architecture. A gap-free spec. When the build started, it was execution — not discovery.

**Sunday, Apr 12 → Friday, Apr 17. Six days. Concept to working language.**

Nights of no sleep, then straight to work the next day. Repeat. Sabbath evenings became build windows — after sundown, after rest, I'd sit down and push.

## The Symbolic Core

Garnet is not just a technical blend. It's a reconciliation:

- **Rust** — rigor, safety, systems integrity, mathematical correctness
- **Ruby** — elegance, expressiveness, human-centered beauty, readability
- **Memory core in the center** — the differentiator. The living heart. First-class memory primitives no other language has.

The logo is half Rust, half Ruby, with the memory core at the intersection. It's right there in the Garnet symbol.

The name itself — *Garnet* — is a gemstone. Formed under pressure. Compressed. Hardened. Beautiful because of the force, not despite it.
